The Evolution of Cancer Research¶

Cancer is currently one of the most well-funded research areas in the world, involving researchers from a wide variety of disciplinary backgrounds - but that hasn't always been the case. I have collected a dataset from the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) using their PubMed database. The PubMed database is perhaps the largest and most widely used collection of biological research. Our dataset includes every scientific research article in their database that includes the word "Cancer" in either the title or the abstract of the article as of Feb 12, 2020, which is about 1.6 million scientific journal articles. If you're interested, you can generate a similar dataset by clicking here.

The dataset includes the title and authors of each article, as well as some data about the publication date, which journal published the article, and some unique identifiers for the paper. Using that information, I'd like to better understand how cancer research has changed over time, and what it looks like today. This is not meant to be a comprehensive study, but I believe that it does provide at least a glimpse into how cancer research as a field has developed over the last 200 years.

With our titles collected, it looks like we actually have some titles that are not in English. Although the cancer research in non-English languages does represent an important part of cancer research history and progress (and may even reveal if non-English researchers study different aspects of cancer), there is a high likelihood that keeping the non-English languages could reduce our confidence in our results by introducing duplicate information that we cannot meaningfully cross-reference (for example, 2 titles might be about identical topics, but being unable to translate the titles means they would get counted separately). Now that we have our set of acceptable words together, let's go ahead and remove the non-English words from our article titles, and also remove common 'stopwords' like "and" or "the". While we're at it, let's also lemmatize our words. "Lemmatization" is essentially a way to normalize our words, for example, changing plurals to singular form, or changing the part of speech of a word. Doing this helps improve our ability to find similarities between titles (although due to the grammatical similarities among article titles, I suspect that lemmatization will not introduce many changes). Together, these processing steps will help us condense our journal article titles down to their key ideas.

There's quite a bit of code here, so I've printed out a before and after version of the first couple of titles, so you can see what they look like before and after these processing steps. The tools here aren't perfect, but it is enough processing to filter out a lot of artifacts from the data so that we can start asking meaningful questions.

# function to convert nltk tag to wordnet tag
def nltk_tag_to_wordnet_tag(nltk_tag):
    if nltk_tag.startswith('J'):
        return wordnet.ADJ
    elif nltk_tag.startswith('V'):
        return wordnet.VERB
    elif nltk_tag.startswith('N'):
        return wordnet.NOUN
    elif nltk_tag.startswith('R'):
        return wordnet.ADV
    else:          
        return None

        # Tokenize the title and get the part of speech of each word

        # Tuple of (token, wordnet_tag) for lemmatizing words
        wordnet_tagged = map(lambda x: (x[0], nltk_tag_to_wordnet_tag(x[1])), nltk_tagged)
        lemmatized_sentence = []
        # For each word and associated part of speech tag in the title
        for word, tag in wordnet_tagged:
            # If there is no available tag, append the token as is
            if tag is None:
                lemmatized_sentence.append(word)
            # If a part of speech tag was obtained, use it to lemmatize the word
            else:        
                lemmatized_sentence.append(lemmatizer.lemmatize(word, tag))
        # Remove stopwords

        # Also, remove tokens that are just numeric values

def get_top_n_trigram(corpus, n=None):
    vec = CountVectorizer(ngram_range=(3, 3)).fit(corpus)
    bag_of_words = vec.transform(corpus)
    sum_words = bag_of_words.sum(axis=0) 
    words_freq = [(word, sum_words[0, idx]) for word, idx in vec.vocabulary_.items()]
    words_freq =sorted(words_freq, key = lambda x: x[1], reverse=True)
    return words_freq[:n]

To further investigate the collaborative nature of research, we can attempt to contruct a social network using the lists of authors. Since recent decades have tens of thousands of journal articles, and each one has several authors, I've chosen to put some restrictions on the authors that we'll include in our social networks, that will hopefully serve as a representative sample of trends in the field.

We will focus our social network analysis on the articles from the 1970's to the 2000's and will only include highly productive researchers that were listed as authors at least 5 times in a decade (averaging 0.5 publications per year) on papers about breast cancer. The logic here is that 1) we've shown breast cancer research accounts for a similar percentage of work in those two decades, 2) limiting to highly productive authors will exclude many students from our analysis, while keeping most well-funded professionals, and 3) authors that have numerous, repeated publications on the same topic are more likely to have worked with other authors that have been studying that topic. This is obviously an incredibly small subset of our data, but it should be big enough to at least give us some information regarding the question we want to answer: How has collaboration and author recognition changed over time?

There's a lot of code here, but it's mostly repetitive, applying the different filters we've described. Ironically, the filtering requires more code than the actual graph creation.

# SOCIAL NETWORK ANALYSIS ATTEMPT
# Large function..
# Input: Decade of interest, and our original data
def createGraph(decade, data):
    # Initialize some variables for keeping track of information
    node_dict = {} # Dictionary of nodes (authors), and how many publications they had in the decade
    edges = [] # List of edges, indicating if 2 authors in out set of nodes have ever co-authored a paper
    G = nx.Graph() # Our social network graph
    articles = [] # A list for holding articles from the given decade
    
    # Create a dictionary that counts author publications about breast cancer in the decade
    for article in data[1:]:
        year = int(article[-1]) # Year of publication
        # If the article was in our decade and is about breast cancer..
        if year - (year%10) == decade and "breast" in article[0].lower():
            # Save the article for further analysis
            articles.append(article)
            # Get the list of authors
            author_list = article[2].split(",") 
            # Fix some formatting, where the final author usually has a period after their name
            if author_list[-1] == ".": 
                author_list[-1] = author_list[-1][0:-1]
            # Increment the count for each author, to keep track of how many breast
            # cancer publications they had in the decade
            for author in author_list: 
                author.strip()
                if author in node_dict.keys():
                    node_dict[author] += 1
                elif "et al" not in author: # Don't allow variations of "et al" to be listed
                    node_dict[author] = 1
                    
    
    # Authors that have studied breast cancer and published at least 5 papers
    # in the decade get added to our graph
    for key in node_dict.keys():
        if node_dict[key] >= 5:
            G.add_node(key)   
                    
    # Iterate through articles from the decade again to find co-authors
    # Only create edges that made it through the filtering and were added to G
    for article in articles:
        year = int(article[-1]) # Publication year
        author_list = article[2].split(",") # Get author list
        if author_list[-1] == ".": #Remove period from last author
            author_list[-1] = author_list[-1][0:-1]
        # Create edges between authors from the article, if both of them 
        # met the filtering criteria to be added to our graph
        for i in range(len(author_list)): 
            for j in range(len(author_list)):
                if i < j:
                    if author_list[i].strip() in G.nodes and author_list[j].strip() in G.nodes:
                        edges.append((author_list[i].strip(), author_list[j].strip()))
                                     
    # Remove duplicate edges - we won't be using a weighted graph here,
    # though that could be interesting as well
    edges_no_dup = list(set([ tuple(sorted(t)) for t in edges ]))
    edges = [list(elem) for elem in edges_no_dup]
    # Add edges to graph
    G.add_edges_from(edges)
    # Save our graph to a file for visualization with Gephi software
    nx.write_gexf(G, str(decade) + "_network.gexf")
    return G

Summary and Conclusion¶

It is no secret that cancer research makes up a significant amount of current medical and biological research. Using data from NCBI's database of journal articles, we've been able to look at information like publication years, article titles, and authors to get a sense for how cancer research has changed over time. Though our analysis is not exhaustive by any means, we've been able to learn several things from our data here, including:

  • Of the 1.6 million cancer articles in the database, over 50% were published in the last decade
  • There has been an increase in the number of cancer articles almost every decade for the last ~200 years
  • Breast and lung cancers have been among the most highly studied cancers over the last 50 years, and together they make up nearly 20% of all cancer research
  • Technology has changed the way cancer research is done, as is evident by the sharp increase in terms like "cell" and "expression" in journal article titles in the last few decades
  • The median number of authors listed on papers is about 5x greater today than it was 50 years ago, possibly indicating an increase in 1) recognition of scientists and 2) collaboration
  • Network analysis reveals that highly productive breast cancer researchers appear as co-others on each others' work much more frequently now than in the past, possibly indicative of increases in collaboration between labs and around the world

These promising findings help reveal how cancer research has developed over the last 2 centuries, and appear to indicate that we are in an era of the greatest collaboration and research output the field has ever seen, likely enhanced by technological advancements that are changing not only how research is performed, but how it is shared.
